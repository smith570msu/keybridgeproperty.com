How Michigan property tax foreclosure works

Michigan operates on a three-year delinquency cycle. When property taxes go unpaid, the county treasurer takes over collection. After three years of delinquency, the county can foreclose and the owner loses the property -- with no right of redemption and no proceeds from the county's subsequent sale.

The critical deadline is March 31 of the foreclosure year. Once that date passes, the county takes title and you receive nothing. A sale before that date -- even a week before -- preserves your equity and stops the process entirely.

Note: tax foreclosure timelines and specific procedures vary by county. Verify current deadlines with your county treasurer or a Michigan real estate attorney before making decisions based on timing.

What happens at closing

When we buy your property, the title company pays all outstanding tax delinquencies, penalties, and interest directly from the sale proceeds before you receive anything. You don't need to come to closing with a check. The delinquency is cleared as part of the transaction, and you walk away with whatever equity remains after the payoff.

Why speed matters here

Options shrink as the deadline approaches. In the early stages of delinquency, you have time to list with an agent, negotiate, and wait for a buyer's financing. Closer to the March 31 foreclosure date, a cash buyer who can close in one to two weeks may be the only realistic option. We've worked with homeowners who had days left before the deadline -- reach out early if you can.

What we need from you

The address, an honest description of the property's condition, and a sense of your timeline. We can look up the tax delinquency amount ourselves from public county records. You don't need to gather paperwork before calling.

Counties we serve

Questions

Straight answers.

Can a sale really happen before the March 31 foreclosure deadline?
Yes, if there's enough lead time. A cash sale can close in as little as one week. The earlier you reach out, the more options you have.
What if I owe more in taxes than the house is worth?
That's a harder situation. Call us and we'll be honest about whether a sale makes sense. In some cases, a deed in lieu or other arrangement may be the better path.
Will selling hurt my credit?
A voluntary sale does not appear on your credit report the way a foreclosure does. Avoiding foreclosure through a sale generally protects your credit significantly.
Do I need to hire an attorney?
We recommend consulting one, especially close to a deadline. We're not attorneys and this isn't legal advice. A Michigan real estate attorney can advise you on your specific situation.
What if my property is already in foreclosure proceedings?
Reach out immediately. We may still be able to act before the final deadline, but time is the critical factor.
